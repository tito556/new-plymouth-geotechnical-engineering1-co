A recent subdivision earthworks project up on the flanks of the Waiwhakaiho catchment had us rethinking the usual compaction specs. The contractor was placing weathered laharic fill—decent material, but it held moisture in odd ways after the morning mist rolled in from the Tasman. Standard site density tests kept flagging borderline results, and the supervising engineer was getting nervous. We pulled samples from three different lift zones and ran a series of Proctor tests under controlled lab conditions to recalibrate the reference curve. What we found was that the material’s optimum moisture content shifted by almost two percentage points between the upper and lower benches. That single adjustment turned a string of failing density tests into a clean QA record for the remainder of the job. In Taranaki, where volcanic-derived soils dominate the landscape, a generic compaction target borrowed from a North Island motorway spec rarely tells the whole story. A proper Proctor test, matched to the actual fill being placed, becomes the linchpin of defensible earthworks documentation—especially when you are working within earshot of the Council’s compliance team. It is the kind of detail that separates a smooth sign-off from a prolonged argument over recompaction costs, and we see that dynamic play out again and again across New Plymouth’s residential and commercial developments.

A Proctor curve calibrated to the actual fill on site is the cheapest insurance against a failed density test and the recompaction that follows.

Approach and scope

The geology under New Plymouth is a layered mix of Egmont-sourced volcaniclastics, alluvial terrace gravels along the Te Henui and Huatoki streams, and pockets of softer sedimentary rock near the coast. Each of these responds differently to compaction effort. Volcanic ash soils, for instance, can exhibit a deceptive crumb structure that breaks down under heavy rolling, shifting the Proctor curve toward higher dry densities but narrower moisture windows. In our experience, running a Modified Proctor on fill derived from the New Plymouth Formation often reveals an optimum moisture content that is wetter than what a field technician expects by feel—leading to over-drying and under-compaction if the lab reference is unavailable. We conduct both Standard and Modified Proctor tests in our ISO 17025-accredited laboratory, following the procedures set out in NZS 4402:1986 and supported by the NZGS guidelines on engineered fill. For projects where the fill source changes during bulk earthworks—something we frequently encounter when a cut transitions from terrace gravel into underlying tephra—the Proctor reference must be updated, not assumed. That adaptability is built into our sampling schedule, and it is often what protects developers from expensive post-construction settlement claims. For deeper stratigraphic control on variable sites, we sometimes pair the Proctor with a test pit investigation to log the transition zones before setting compaction targets.

Site-specific factors

New Plymouth’s average annual rainfall nudges past 1,400 mm, with the heaviest falls concentrated between May and September. That persistent moisture is the single biggest enemy of engineered fill placement, because a Proctor curve is only a reliable reference if the moisture-density relationship was established on material that represents what is actually being compacted in the cut. When a contractor pushes fill during a wet winter and the lab curve was built from summer-dry samples, the field density readings can drift into a fiction. We have audited projects where the specified relative compaction was being met on paper, but the fill was actually being placed several percent wet of optimum—creating a risk of post-construction settlement and, in the worst cases, slope instability on steeper graded platforms. The interaction between Proctor data and slope stability analysis becomes critical on hillside subdivisions around Frankleigh Park and Burgess Park, where cut-to-fill transitions concentrate drainage and impose higher shear stresses on compacted layers. Getting the Proctor reference right is not just about passing a nuclear gauge test; it is about ensuring that the placed fill performs over the full design life of the structure it supports.

Relevant standards

Related technical services

01

Standard Proctor (NZS 4402 Method 4.1)

The lower-energy compaction test used for residential platforms, landscaping fills, and lightly loaded footings. Suitable for silty and clayey soils typical of Taranaki’s weathered volcanic profiles.

02

Modified Proctor (NZS 4402 Method 4.2)

Higher compactive effort intended for road subgrade, structural fill under commercial slabs, and heavy-duty pavement layers. Often specified by New Plymouth District Council for arterial roadworks.

03

Moisture-Density Curve Recalibration

When fill sources change mid-project, we re-sample and re-run the Proctor curve to update the QA reference. Prevents the common error of applying an obsolete curve to new material.

04

Field Density Correlation (Sand Cone/Nuclear Gauge)

We pair lab Proctor data with sand cone density testing on site to calculate relative compaction. This closes the loop between the laboratory reference and what the roller actually achieves.

Common questions

How much does a Proctor test cost in New Plymouth?

For a standard one-point or full five-point Proctor curve, laboratory fees in the Taranaki region typically range between NZ$170 and NZ$360. The final figure depends on whether you need a Standard or Modified Proctor, how many points are required to define the curve, and whether oversize corrections apply. We provide a fixed quote once we know the fill material and project specification.

When should we update the Proctor reference during earthworks?

Update the Proctor curve whenever the fill source changes—visually, by location, or by depth. In New Plymouth, we commonly see this when a cut transitions from brown volcanic ash into grey alluvial silts or when imported hardfill is introduced mid-project. A change in soil classification (per NZGS field description) is the trigger. Running a new Proctor at that point ensures your field density tests remain meaningful and defensible.

What is the difference between Standard and Modified Proctor for Taranaki soils?

The fundamental difference is the compactive effort: Modified Proctor delivers roughly 4.5 times more energy per unit volume than Standard Proctor. For Taranaki’s silty volcanic soils, Modified Proctor typically yields a maximum dry density 5–10% higher and an optimum moisture content 2–4 percentage points lower than the Standard curve. The choice depends on the design specification—residential fills often use Standard, while road subgrades and structural platforms under commercial buildings usually require Modified.
