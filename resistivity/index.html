In New Plymouth, we often see contractors surprised by how quickly subsurface conditions change between the volcanic lahar deposits near the mountain and the coastal sands along the Tasman. The variable saturation and clay content in these layers make resistivity surveys a practical first step before any intrusive drilling. Our team applies Vertical Electrical Sounding to map depth transitions, locate the water table, and identify low-resistivity zones that can indicate soft or contaminated ground. For deeper bedrock profiling where VES reaches its practical limit, we combine the survey with seismic refraction to cross-validate the top-of-rock and weathering profile. Every field campaign around Taranaki Maunga demands an awareness of the region's high annual rainfall pushing groundwater close to surface and altering apparent resistivity readings.

Resistivity alone does not replace a borehole, but it tells you exactly where to drill and what to expect before the rig arrives.

Approach and scope

Comparing the Fitzroy coastal strip to the Vogeltown hillside illustrates why array configuration matters. Along Fitzroy, the shallow sand aquifer overlies a silty confining layer, and a Wenner array with tight electrode spacing can separate the freshwater lens from the underlying saline intrusion effectively. Up in Vogeltown, where weathered andesite sits beneath residual clay, a Schlumberger array with expanding current electrodes provides better vertical resolution to define the rockhead. We always tie our resistivity profiles to borehole logs when available, calibrating the electrical response to the actual lithology.
  • Wenner array for horizontal layering and lateral continuity checks
  • Schlumberger array for deeper penetration and vertical resolution
  • Dipole-dipole for 2D imaging across proposed cut faces
The resulting inverted sections give engineers a continuous picture of the subsurface, reducing the guesswork between boreholes and helping target the most representative locations for SPT drilling.

Site-specific factors

New Plymouth sits at roughly 39° south, exposed to Tasman Sea weather systems that saturate the ground for much of the year. High soil moisture lowers bulk resistivity, which can mask the contrast between a clean gravel and a clayey silt if the survey is run immediately after heavy rain. We schedule resistivity lines with a dry-weather window whenever possible and record soil moisture conditions at the time of acquisition. The district's volcanic geology introduces another risk: andesite boulders within lahar deposits create high-resistivity anomalies that can be misread as bedrock highs. Our processing workflow includes topographic correction and lateral constraint from existing borehole data to avoid these false interpretations. Without this calibration step, a designer might overestimate bearing capacity at a bridge abutment or underestimate the depth to competent rock for a pile socket, leading to costly redesign mid-construction.

Related technical services

01

Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

One-dimensional depth sounding using Schlumberger array to map layer thicknesses and resistivity contrasts at a single location. Ideal for groundwater exploration and bedrock depth estimation.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

Multi-electrode profiling along a line to produce a continuous resistivity cross-section. Used for cavity detection, pipeline route assessment, and landslide investigation in Taranaki's hill country.

03

Soil Corrosivity Assessment

Resistivity measurements combined with pH and redox potential to classify soil aggressiveness toward buried steel and concrete per NZS 3404 and AS/NZS 2041.

Common questions

How much does an electrical resistivity survey cost in New Plymouth?

A typical VES sounding or short 2D resistivity line in the New Plymouth district runs between NZ$1,190 and NZ$1,930, depending on line length, electrode count, and site access. Steep terrain or remote sites outside the city may push costs toward the upper end due to extra labour and setup time.

Can resistivity surveys tell the difference between fresh and saline groundwater near the coast?

Yes, and this is one of the most common applications along the New Plymouth foreshore. Saline water has very low resistivity, typically below 5 Ω·m, while fresh groundwater in the coastal sands reads above 30 Ω·m. The contrast is sharp enough that we can map the freshwater-saltwater interface with decent precision using a Wenner array.

How deep can Vertical Electrical Sounding reach in Taranaki's volcanic terrain?

Practical depth depends on the maximum current electrode separation (AB/2) we can lay out on site. With AB/2 of 200 metres, we routinely reach 60 to 80 metres depth in the lahar and ash deposits around New Plymouth. In dry summer conditions over compacted andesite, the penetration may reduce slightly, but we can still resolve the weathering profile and top-of-rock to depths that cover most foundation and retaining wall designs.

Do I still need boreholes if I run a resistivity survey?

Almost always, yes. Resistivity gives you a continuous image and highlights anomalous zones, but it does not provide direct strength parameters or sample recovery. The smart approach is to run the resistivity lines first, then place boreholes at locations that the survey flags as representative or suspicious. This combination cuts the total investigation cost and improves confidence in the ground model.

What site conditions interfere with resistivity measurements?

Buried metallic pipes, reinforced concrete slabs, overhead power lines, and grounded fences all distort the electrical field and can produce artefacts in the inverted section. In New Plymouth's older industrial areas around Port Taranaki, we map surface infrastructure before laying out the array and often offset the line to avoid interference. Very dry surface soils also create high contact resistance; we wet the electrode positions or use bentonite paste to maintain reliable current injection.
